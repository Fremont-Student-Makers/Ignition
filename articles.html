Simple questions

Short answers that open into a real article when you need more detail.

Flight Will this rocket work? Check stability, motor fit, and recovery first.

TLDR: Simulate it before you build too far, because a rocket can look fine and still fail on stability, motor choice, or recovery sizing.

Use the simulation page to check whether the rocket is stable, whether the motor is a reasonable match, and whether the recovery system is sized for the expected altitude. If one of those pieces is off, the design may still fly, but not in the way you expect.

Flight path Why didn't my rocket fly straight? More than one thing can cause a crooked flight.

TLDR: Don’t stop at fin alignment. A rocket can arc, weathercock, or veer because of wind, rod angle, weight balance, a bent launch lug, fin warping, or a motor that is not a good fit.

Start by checking launch conditions, then inspect the rocket itself. A strong crosswind can push a rocket off course right after launch, especially if the rod or rail was angled into the wind. After that, look for asymmetry: fins that are slightly warped, a nose weight problem, a crooked launch lug, uneven glue blobs, or damage from an earlier flight.

If the rocket still turns one direction every time, simulation and launch setup are the fastest ways to narrow it down. If the issue changes from flight to flight, the launch field or motor choice is often the better suspect.

Legal Is it legal to launch this? Local rules, field rules, and airspace rules all matter.

TLDR: The answer depends on where you are launching, what motor or size you are using, and whether the site or club allows it.

Check local aviation guidance, club safety codes, and the rules for the field you want to use. A legal launch in one region may be restricted in another, and some places require extra notifications or club supervision. If you are unsure, use a club or approved launch field instead of guessing.

Motor choice What motor should I use? Choose the motor for the rocket and the field, not the biggest one.

TLDR: Match the motor to the rocket’s size, weight, and experience level, then simulate the result before you buy or launch.

A bigger motor is not automatically a better motor. If the rocket is too light, too small, or too fragile, more thrust can make the flight harder to predict. A motor that fits the design and the field usually gives a cleaner first flight.

Recovery Why did it come down hard? The recovery system may be wrong for the flight.

TLDR: Hard landings usually come from an under-sized parachute, a packed recovery system, a weak ejection event, or a rocket that flew higher than the recovery setup was designed for.

Check whether the parachute or streamer was packed correctly, whether the ejection charge worked, and whether the rocket was overpowered for the recovery setup. A rocket that reaches a higher altitude than expected may descend too fast if the recovery hardware is too small.

Building What glue should I use? Different joints need different glue types.

TLDR: Use white glue or wood glue for beginner kits, CA for small fast fixes, and epoxy for stronger high-stress joints.

If you are building a simple kit, the safest answer is usually the simplest one. White glue and wood glue give you enough working time for alignment and are easy to sand. CA is useful for small parts when you need speed, and epoxy is best when the joint has to carry more load.

Advanced When is it too advanced? If the basics are still shaky, wait.

TLDR: If you are still learning to build, launch, or simulate safely, active control is probably too early.

Active control and competition projects ask more of the builder, the launch field, and the recovery system. That is why the site keeps those paths separate. If the current project depends on sensors, control surfaces, or very specific launch conditions, make sure the basic workflow already feels routine.
